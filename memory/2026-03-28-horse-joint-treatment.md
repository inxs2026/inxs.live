assistant: [[reply_to_current]] That explains it perfectly. Pigeon-toed (toed-in) is a textbook cause of lateral branch suspensory stress — and it lines up exactly with what the ultrasound found.

**Why pigeon-toed causes this:**
- When a toed-in horse breaks over, the foot wings **outward** at every stride
- That outward rotation puts asymmetric load on the **lateral** suspensory branch
- Every single step — training, racing, even walking in the paddock — is stressing that same attachment point
- Bilateral because both feet are toed-in, lateral worse because that's the branch taking the twisting force

---

**Shoeing is your most powerful tool here:**

**Goal:** Encourage a straighter breakover, reduce the lateral twisting

- **Square or rolled toe** — shortens breakover, reduces the winging motion
- **Lateral extension or wider web on the lateral side** — supports the outside of the foot
- **Heel support** — don't let the heels collapse (toed-in horses often have accompanying heel imbalance)
- Some farriers use a **lateral trailer** on the shoe to correct the flight path

A farrier experienced with sport/racehorses who understands rotational limb deformities is worth every penny here. This isn't a standard reset job.

---

**What you can't change:**
- The conformation itself is set in an adult horse
- You're managing mechanics, not correcting the underlying structure

**What this means long-term:**
- He's **raceable and manageable** — pigeon-toed horses run and win all the time
- Your edge is being ahead of it: right farrier + proactive shockwave + monitoring
- The lateral branches will always be his vulnerability — that's just his body
